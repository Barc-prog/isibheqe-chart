The IsiBheqe soHlamvu syllabary

A simple explainer page with interactive chart

IsiBheqe soHlamvu is a modern, phonetically-based indigenous writing system developed for all Southern African isiNtu languages. It is also known as Ditema tsa Dinoko, or Xifhungo xa Manungu, or Vhuga ha Madungo.

IsiBheqe soHlamvu roughly means "the ibheqe-style syllable" or "the ibheqe method of writing syllables".

IsiBheqe/Ditema draws from Southern African traditional art, like Sesotho litema art (used on murals) and isiZulu ucu art (used on beadwork bracelets). This art is decorative; but used systems of shapes to convey romantic messages, or report cultural news to neighbours and travelers. Different shapes indicated births, childrens' coming-of-age milestones and the like.

In that tradition, IsiBheqe/Ditema is a communicative cultural technology, that can be used as an 'art style'.

The script can be used for all Southern African isiNtu languages & dialects. It is especially useful for those languages that don't have a formal Latin orthography. These include the Eastern Sotho language of sePulana and a majority of the Tekela languages of the Nguni language group like isiBhaca, isiHlubi, sePhuthi, isiLala, isiNhlangwini, and Northern Transvaal isiNdebele (also known as Sumayela Ndebele).

The isiBheqe/Ditema writing system - the basic idea

The Isibheqe/Ditema script is a featural syllabary

It's a syllabary because each symbol stands for a 'syllable' rather than a single letter. It's featural because each symbol is designed to show how the syllable sounds. Its phonetic features are built right into its shape.

An individual symbol is called an ibheqe (plural: amabheqe). Every ibheqe has a triangle-shaped nucleus in the middle. This shows the vowel.
The 'nucleus' resembles a mouth. Then, marks and shapes are positioned around this nucleus to indicate the consonants sounds. These are positioned so they mimic where and how the cononant sounds are made in the mouth. Where necessary, other markings can show additional features like articulatory mode which can further distingush consonants. Examples of articulatory mode include vibration of vocal cords, or source of airflow.



Vowels

In IsiBheqe, the vowel is shown by the the direction of the triangle inside the symbol.
This direction mirrors how the vowel is physically made.

High and Low vowels:

  • The vowels i and u are high vowels — the jaw moves upward to make these sounds.
    → They’re shown with an upward-pointing triangle.
    • i uses a solid triangle.
    • u uses an upward-pointing chevron shape.


  • The vowel a is a low vowel — the jaw lowers.
    → It’s shown with a downward-pointing triangle.

Front and Back vowels:

  • e and ɛ are front vowels, made near the front of the mouth.
  • o and ɔ are back vowels, made toward the back of the mouth.

Thus, vowel direction in IsiBheqe maps visually to the shape of the mouth and tongue when pronouncing it.



Consonants

If the vowel triangle represents the vocal tract, then the consonant features show what happens inside it.

  • Place of articulation — where in the mouth the sound is formed (lips, teeth, palate, velum, etc.).
  • Manner of articulation — how the airflow is shaped (plosive, fricative, nasal, click, etc.).

    • Beyond place and manner, sounds differ in how the air moves
  • Articulatory mode — for example, whether the airflow is steady, stopped, or burst out sharply (as in ejectives).
