The IsiBheqe soHlamvu Syllabary

A simple explainer page with interactive chart

↓ Skip to interactive chart

IsiBheqe soHlamvu is a modern, phonetically-based indigenous writing system developed for all Southern African isiNtu languages. It is also known as Ditema tsa Dinoko, Xifhungo xa Manungu, or Vhuga ha Madungo.

▸ Background & context

IsiBheqe/Ditema draws from Southern African traditional art, like Sesotho litema art (used on murals) and isiZulu ucu art (used on beadwork). This art was decorative but also used systems of shapes to convey messages—births, milestones, news for neighbors and travelers.

The script can be used for all Southern African isiNtu languages and dialects. It's especially useful for languages without formal Latin orthography, like sePulana, isiBhaca, isiHlubi, sePhuthi, isiLala, isiNhlangwini, and Northern Transvaal isiNdebele.

Not letter by letter — one symbol, one complete sound unit
pʰa

In English you write one letter per sound: b-a-l-l = 4 letters. IsiBheqe works differently — one symbol captures a full syllable: vowel and consonant bundled together.

A single symbol is called an ibheqe (plural: amabheqe). The symbol to the left is the ibheqe for pʰa — the syllable in pha, meaning "give" in isiZulu and Sesotho.

Every ibheqe has two parts: a triangle in the middle (the vowel), and marks around it (the consonant). The next two steps explain each.
The triangle is the vowel
Its direction mirrors where your jaw actually goes

At the centre of every ibheqe is a triangle — the nucleus. The direction it points encodes the vowel, because it maps directly onto where your jaw goes when you make that sound. High vowels (jaw up) point upward. Low vowels (jaw drops) point down.

👆 Click any of the big triangles with a vowel inside it to hear it

 

Try it: say i (as in impi) — feel your jaw close. Now say a (as in amanzi) — feel it drop. That physical difference is what the triangle direction encodes.

The marks show the consonant
Where in your mouth, how the air moves, and which exact version

Articulatory modes — a closer look Optional

The walkthrough showed you where and how — this section zooms in on the extra marks that tell you which version of a sound you're looking at. Same place, same shape, different release.

① Voicing — the line down the middle

Your vocal cords can buzz (voiced) or stay quiet (voiceless) for any consonant. In the symbol, a small filled dot means voiced — you can feel it as a continuous buzz in your throat from the first moment of the sound.

Try it: Put your hand on your throat. Say "pha" — no buzz at the start. Now say "bha" — the buzz starts immediately.

② Ejectives — the dot/lerothodi/iliconsi

English doesn't have ejectives, but they're common in isiZulu, Sesotho, and Setswana. It's a stop with an extra sharp burst — instead of air from the lungs alone, the closed throat pushes up a quick extra pulse of pressure.

Try it: Say "pha" normally. Now hold your breath tight, close your lips, and release sharply — that popping sensation is close to an ejective.

For isiNtu speakers: You already make ejectives naturally — they're the "sharp" stops in words like phakathi (isiZulu) or tshela (Sesotho). The lerothodi marks that version of the sound.
③ Implosives

An implosive looks voiced — and it is — but there's a subtle extra quality. As the lips close and release, air briefly draws inward before the burst outward. It gives the sound a slightly hollow, sunken quality.

Note for English speakers: The English "b" in "banana" is actually closer to an implosive than a plain voiced stop. In isiNtu languages, bha is the plain voiced stop (like English "b"), and ba is the true implosive — subtly different, but your ear will catch it with the audio.

④ Prenasalisation — the ingungwana

A nasal hum that flows directly into the consonant — so fast it sounds like one sound, not two. The ingungwana (nasalisation circle) sits at the apex of the triangle, because nasals live at the front of the mouth.

Try it: Say "umbrella" slowly — feel the "mm" flowing straight into the "b"? Now speed it up until the two sounds fuse. That's prenasalisation: mba, nda, nga in isiNtu are single onset sounds, not clusters.
