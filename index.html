The IsiBheqe soHlamvu syllabary

A simple explainer page with interactive chart

IsiBheqe soHlamvu is a modern, phonetically-based indigenous writing system developed for all Southern African isiNtu languages. It is also known as Ditema tsa Dinoko, or Xifhungo xa Manungu, or Vhuga ha Madungo.

IsiBheqe soHlamvu roughly means "the ibheqe-style syllable" or "the ibheqe method of writing syllables".

IsiBheqe/Ditema draws from Southern African traditional art, like Sesotho litema art (used on murals) and isiZulu ucu art (used on beadwork bracelets). This art is decorative; but used systems of shapes to convey romantic messages, or report cultural news to neighbours and travelers. Different shapes indicated births, childrens' coming-of-age milestones and the like.

In that tradition, IsiBheqe/Ditema is a communicative cultural technology, that can be used as an 'art style'.

The script can be used for all Southern African isiNtu languages & dialects. It is especially useful for those languages that don't have a formal Latin orthography. These include the Eastern Sotho language of sePulana and a majority of the Tekela languages of the Nguni language group like isiBhaca, isiHlubi, sePhuthi, isiLala, isiNhlangwini, and Northern Transvaal isiNdebele (also known as Sumayela Ndebele).

BASIC IDEA OF THE ISIBHEQE/DITEMA SCRIPT

The Isibheqe/Ditema writing system is a featural syllabary

It is a syllabary because its symbols stand for 'syllables' - not letters.

It is featural because a symbol is designed to visually show you how its syllable is pronounced - i.e. show you the syllable's phonetic features.

An isiBheqe symbol is known as an ibheqe, or amabheqe, in plural.

The vowel 'nucleus' and the consonants

Which features are shown? One is the vowel, which is visually shown by the direction the triangle or chevron is pointing.

The triangle is the 'nucleus' of an ibheqe. Visually, it is meant to look like a mouth (or more accurately a vocal tract - which includes the mouth, nose & throat). The apex of the triangle is the front of the mouth (lips) with the base of the triangle being the throat.

Then, features of the syllable's consonant sounds - like 'where' and 'how' the consonant is pronounced in the mouth (vocal tract) - are shown, in corresponding places on the triangle. Linguists call these features the "place of articulation" and the "manner of articulation" respectively.

Finally, the ibheqe can also show the "articulatory mode" of a sound. This is the type of airflow used to pronounce the sound.



VOWELS

The vowel shapes in isiBheqe

In the IsiBheqe/Ditema writing system, the vowel is shown by the the DIRECTION of the triangle in the middle.

This direction is based on how the vowel is articulated.

HIGH AND LOW VOWELS:

The vowels i and u are 'high' vowels.

'High' vowels are so named because the jaw moves UPWARD when you make the sound. Since isiBheqe is a featural script, 'high' vowels are visually shown using an 'upward' pointing triangle.

Specifically, the i vowel is an upward-pointing TRIANGLE.
The vowel u is an upward-pointing CHEVRON.

The 'low' vowel a is a 'low' vowel.
DOWNWARD pointing triangle.

FRONTED VOWELS: The vowels e and ɛ are called 'front' vowels, because you make them at the front of the mouth.

Back vowels The vowels o and ɔ are 'back' vowels, because you make them at the back of the mouth.





CONSONANTS

Place of articulation

Recall, the vowel triangle resembles the 'vocal tract'.

Inside the vocal tract, sits the body parts one uses to make speech sounds. These parts are called "articulators".
They include your lips, teeth, gums, tongue, roof of your mouth, throat, vocal cords, nose etc.
